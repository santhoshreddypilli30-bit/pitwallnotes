Rebuilt Overnight, Broken at Turn 6: How Two Crashes Decided a Chaotic Belgian Grand Prix

Kimi Antonelli won a fraught Belgian Grand Prix at Spa-Francorchamps after his mechanics rebuilt his teammate's practice-crashed car overnight, a first-lap collision at Turn 6 sent George Russell into the gravel and triggered an early Safety Car, and Max Verstappen charged to the podium on an older-specification rear wing. Lewis Hamilton took a five-second penalty for the Russell contact — and still ended the day having leapfrogged his own teammate in the championship.

The overnight rebuild: how Fagnes Chicane wrecked two teams' weekends before the race even started

Spa-Francorchamps had already delivered its share of drama before a single competitive lap of the Grand Prix had been run. In practice, Lewis Hamilton and Pierre Gasly both crashed at the Fagnes Chicane — the tight, off-camber left-right flick in the middle sector that has claimed cars for decades. The damage was serious enough that Ferrari and Alpine mechanics spent the gap between practice and qualifying stripping and rebuilding chassis components, working through the night to have both cars fit to run. It's an unglamorous but critical piece of the engineering story: neither team's raceday pace can be understood without acknowledging that both cars turned up to Saturday having been reassembled, not simply prepared.

Layered on top of that was a wave of power-unit grid penalties. Five drivers — Fernando Alonso, Isack Hadjar, Carlos Sainz, Lance Stroll and Lando Norris — took on new engine components beyond their season allocation and dropped grid places as a result. Norris's case stung the most: he qualified third on merit, only to be shuffled back ten places to P13 by the penalty. Hadjar's was more severe still, a 20-place drop that buried him on the back row alongside Alonso before a wheel had even turned in anger.

Pirelli had gone into the weekend expecting thermal degradation to push most of the field toward two stops. But the cooler-than-forecast track temperatures that built up across the weekend changed the calculation enough that, when the tyre blankets came off on the grid, the bulk of the field lined up on the medium compound rather than the soft everyone had planned around. Only Valtteri Bottas and Sainz gambled on the red-walled soft, while Norris, Sergio Perez, Stroll, Hadjar and Alonso — several of them already out of position from grid penalties — started on the hard. It meant the tyre story of the race was being rewritten before the lights even went out.

Qualifying: Antonelli's 1:44.361 beats Verstappen by three tenths

None of the overnight repair work stopped Kimi Antonelli from putting his Mercedes on pole. A 1:44.361 was enough to head Max Verstappen's Red Bull by 0.317 seconds, giving Mercedes the outright fastest car through Spa's long, flowing middle sector. Norris's third-fastest lap would have completed a strong front three, but his grid penalty meant the effective front row for Sunday was Antonelli and Verstappen — a Mercedes-Red Bull duel with both Ferraris and the recovering McLaren waiting behind.

Lap 1, Eau Rouge: Verstappen's launch, and Antonelli's answer down Kemmel

Antonelli made a clean getaway but had to sit and wait on the grid while the other 21 cars settled — a small procedural wrinkle that did nothing to slow him once the five red lights went out. Verstappen got the better launch of the front two and swept past into the lead at Eau Rouge, Spa's most famous corner, in what looked for a moment like the platform for a Red Bull victory. It didn't last: Antonelli used Mercedes's straight-line advantage down the Kemmel Straight to reclaim first place before the cars had even reached Les Combes.

Further back, Charles Leclerc made the most of a strong start to move up to third, as George Russell — who had qualified well down on his Mercedes teammate — lost ground and slipped behind both Ferraris.

Lap 1, Turn 6: the collision that ended Russell's race and shaped the title fight

Looking to recover the places he'd lost at the start, Russell tried to come back at Lewis Hamilton into Turn 6. The two made contact; Russell was pitched across the track and beached his Mercedes in the gravel trap, his race over before the opening lap was even finished. Hamilton's explanation over team radio was that he had been "right behind another car so had no front end" — a description of the classic dirty-air problem, where a car running in the turbulent wake of the one ahead loses front-end downforce and, with it, the ability to judge a braking zone or a closing gap accurately.

"Right behind another car so had no front end."

— Lewis Hamilton, Ferrari, on the contact with George Russell at Turn 6

The stewards handed Hamilton a five-second time penalty for his part in the collision — later served in the pits — and confirmed a separate post-race investigation into an unsafe release from his own pit box, a second procedural question mark hanging over Ferrari's afternoon. Russell's retirement, still only lap one, immediately reshaped the championship picture: a driver fighting at the front of the standings was about to leave Spa with nothing.

The incident wasn't isolated. In the same opening laps, Esteban Ocon made contact twice — first with Carlos Sainz, then with his own Haas teammate Oliver Bearman — leaving Sainz with a front-left puncture and Ocon himself sliding to the back of the field. Between Russell's crash and the Ocon-Sainz-Bearman tangle, race control had more than enough reason to bring out the Safety Car before the first lap was even complete.

The early Safety Car: who gambled, who waited

With the Safety Car out and the field bunched, several teams took the opportunity for a "free" pit stop while the pace was neutralised — Hadjar, Bearman and both Cadillacs of Perez and Bottas all came in for fresh rubber. Antonelli made the braver call at the front: pitting early from the race lead, trusting that his in-lap and out-lap would keep him ahead of Leclerc and Verstappen once the sequence shook out. It worked. When racing resumed at the start of Lap 5 through La Source, Antonelli still led.

The restart itself produced an immediate skirmish: Hamilton nosed ahead of Oscar Piastri into La Source, only for the McLaren driver to retake the position a few hundred metres later. Further back, Racing Bulls were enjoying an excellent opening stint, with Liam Lawson and Arvid Lindblad running sixth and seventh and locked in a three-way scrap with Alpine's Franco Colapinto — a strong recovery drive from a team that would go on to log its fifth consecutive double-points weekend.

Red Bull's old-spec gamble: Verstappen's podium on last year's rear wing

The most quietly impressive engineering story of the afternoon belonged to Red Bull. Running an older-specification rear wing rather than the team's latest aerodynamic package, Verstappen still found the pace to pass Leclerc at Les Combes and briefly move into second place, closing on race leader Antonelli. It's a reminder that outright downforce numbers on a spec sheet don't always translate directly to track performance — driver confidence in a familiar aero balance, tyre management, and how a car rides Spa's high-speed compressions can matter just as much as which wing is bolted on. Red Bull extracted a podium from a car that, on paper, wasn't running its most current specification.

Exactly how Ferrari reclaimed second place before the flag wasn't detailed in the lap-by-lap coverage available at the time of writing, but the final gap between Leclerc and Verstappen — 9.6 seconds — suggests Ferrari's pace over the closing stint, rather than a single overtaking move, is what settled the podium order.

The finish: Antonelli resists a late Safety Car restart and a Leclerc charge

Antonelli's afternoon wasn't a comfortable cruise to the flag. He had to see off the pressure of the earlier Safety Car restart and then hold off a late charge from Leclerc, who closed right up to the back of the Mercedes in the closing laps. Antonelli kept his composure and took the chequered flag by 1.952 seconds — his sixth win of the 2026 season, and his first since the Monaco Grand Prix. Verstappen completed the podium in third, 11.586 seconds back, ahead of a Hamilton who crossed the line fourth with his five-second penalty already absorbed into his pit stop, and Piastri fifth.

Points from nowhere: Hadjar and Norris recover from the back

If the front of the race belonged to Mercedes, the story further back belonged to the two drivers hit hardest by grid penalties. Isack Hadjar, who started at the back of the field after his 20-place drop, carved his way to a sixth-place finish — arguably the standout individual drive of the weekend given where he started. Lando Norris, who had out-qualified both Ferraris only to be sent to P13 by his own penalty, recovered to finish seventh, just shy of the top-six target he'd set himself before the race. Between them, the pair turned two of the weekend's harshest regulatory penalties into a reminder that Spa's long straights and flowing corners reward a car that can pass cleanly — something both the Red Bull-powered Racing Bulls and the updated McLaren clearly could do on this occasion.

Championship arithmetic: Hamilton leapfrogs his own teammate despite the penalty

The most consequential number from Spa isn't Antonelli's winning margin — it's what Russell's zero did to the order behind him. Going into the weekend, Russell held second in the standings on 154 points, seven ahead of Hamilton's 147. Russell's retirement scored him nothing, while Hamilton's fourth place — worth 12 points even with the five-second penalty folded into his pit stop — was enough to push him to 159 points. By our count, that puts Hamilton ahead of Russell in the standings for the first time this season, despite picking up a penalty and a post-race investigation on the very same afternoon. Antonelli's win stretches his own lead at the top to 204 points, with Leclerc's second place moving the Ferrari driver up to 126.

Between an overnight chassis rebuild, a first-lap collision that ended a title contender's afternoon before it began, an old-spec rear wing outperforming expectations, and a penalty that couldn't stop a championship swing, Spa lived up to its reputation as the calendar's most unpredictable venue. For Antonelli, the result is simple: a sixth win, and a championship lead that keeps growing even on the weekends where he isn't the one making headlines for the wrong reasons. For Mercedes as a team, it's more complicated — a win and a DNF from the same two cars, on the same afternoon, at the same corner where their title challenge briefly came apart.
