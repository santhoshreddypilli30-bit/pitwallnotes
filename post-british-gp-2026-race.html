Wheel Shield, Rear Wing, Software Bug: How Three Failures Decided the British Grand Prix

Charles Leclerc won his first Silverstone Grand Prix after Kimi Antonelli's front-wheel-shield failure and four track-limits infringements dropped the polesitter to 16th, Max Verstappen's late rear-wing failure triggered the safety car, and an FIA software error denied the crowd a green-flag finish.

Silverstone's tyre trap: why Pirelli brought its three hardest compounds

If the Sprint on Saturday was decided by energy deployment, Sunday's 52-lap Grand Prix was shaped by a different constraint entirely: tyre degradation. Silverstone's National layout combines high-speed direction changes through Maggotts, Becketts and Stowe with heavy lateral loading that punishes tyre sidewalls harder than almost any other circuit on the calendar. Pirelli's response was to bring the three hardest compounds in its entire 2026 range — the C1 as the hard, C2 as the medium, and C3 as the soft — the same conservative selection the circuit has received in recent seasons.

Pirelli's simulations pointed clearly to a one-stop race, with a medium-to-hard strategy calculated to be roughly 13 seconds quicker over the full distance than committing to a second stop. That number matters: it meant no front-running team had a strategic incentive to pit twice unless something outside their control — a Safety Car, a puncture, a mechanical failure — changed the calculation. As it turned out, Sunday delivered all three of those disruptions before the flag.

Qualifying: Antonelli takes pole back from Hamilton, Russell recovers from a Q1 scare

Having lost Friday's sprint pole to Lewis Hamilton by 0.011 seconds, Kimi Antonelli responded on Saturday afternoon by taking Grand Prix pole with a 1:28.111, beating Charles Leclerc's Ferrari by 0.175 seconds. Hamilton lined up third, still carrying the pace that had briefly troubled Mercedes all weekend. George Russell had a scare of his own, making a mistake in Q1 that threatened an early exit, before recovering to qualify fourth. Isack Hadjar took a strong fifth for Red Bull, with Lando Norris sixth for McLaren.

On pure one-lap pace, the front two rows suggested a straight fight between Mercedes and Ferrari for the win — and for the opening lap, that is exactly what it looked like.

Lap 1: Leclerc takes the lead into Turn 1

Antonelli's pole lap counted for little once the lights went out. Leclerc got the better launch and moved into the lead at Turn 1, immediately putting Ferrari in control of the race rather than reacting to Mercedes. It was the first sign of what would become a recurring theme of the afternoon: a strong Saturday result for Mercedes failing to convert into control of Sunday's race.

Further back, Oscar Piastri's race unravelled almost immediately — an opening-lap incident left the McLaren needing a pit stop for damage before the first lap was even complete, a deficit he would never recover from, eventually finishing 11th and outside the points.

Lap 7: Hamilton's false start and a five-second penalty

Seven laps in, the stewards opened an investigation into Lewis Hamilton for a starting-procedure infringement. Replay footage showed his Ferrari creeping forward before the five lights went out, before he managed to check the car and restart correctly on the actual signal — enough movement to be judged a false start under the regulations. The stewards confirmed a five-second time penalty, which Ferrari opted to serve during Hamilton's first scheduled pit stop rather than take as a stop-go, folding the sanction into the one-stop plan already in motion.

It meant Hamilton's afternoon carried a five-second handicap from very early on — one that would matter enormously by the time the chequered flag fell under circumstances nobody at Silverstone expected.

Lap 41: "something is broken" — Antonelli's wheel-shield failure

While Leclerc controlled the race from the front and Hamilton and Russell shadowed him, Antonelli had been quietly recovering ground after losing the lead at the start. That recovery ended on Lap 41, when his Mercedes radio carried a message that the car was no longer behaving normally. The team's read was a left-front wheel-shield failure — a smaller aerodynamic component, but one whose detachment measurably affects front-end airflow and, in Antonelli's case, made the car reluctant to turn in cleanly.

Mercedes brought him in, but the fix didn't resolve the handling problem, and a second additional stop followed shortly after — two unplanned visits to the pits that a strict one-stop race can rarely absorb without losing serious track position.

"It was going from bad to worse."

— Kimi Antonelli, Mercedes, on the wheel-shield failure and its aftermath

Lap 44: a fourth track-limits infringement ends the podium fight

Compounding the mechanical issue, Antonelli ran off track at Turn 6 on Lap 44 without what the stewards considered a justifiable reason — his fourth track-limits infringement of the race. Under the FIA's standard penalty guidelines, a fourth breach carries an automatic five-second time penalty. The stewards noted they accepted Antonelli was managing a genuine mechanical problem, but ruled that did not excuse repeatedly running beyond the white line. Mercedes chose not to contest the decision after reviewing it internally, effectively conceding that the sheer volume of off-track moments left no real case to answer.

Between the wheel-shield failure, the extra pit stops, and the penalty, Antonelli's polesitter's afternoon ended in 16th — outside the points entirely, on a weekend where he had led both the sprint and the championship coming in.

Lap 48: Verstappen's rear wing and a late safety car

With 52 laps due to run their course under a straightforward one-stop script, the race's final complication arrived from a third team entirely. Running third and looking set for a solid points finish, Max Verstappen went off at Stowe on Lap 48 and beached his Red Bull in the gravel trap, ending his afternoon on the spot. Verstappen blamed a rear-wing failure for the sudden loss of control — a claim consistent with the abruptness of the accident, though not one that had been independently confirmed by the team by the time this was written.

The stranded car brought out the Safety Car with four laps remaining, compressing the field and handing Ferrari and Mercedes a final round of strategic decisions with almost no time to make them.

A high-speed circuit corner — the kind of run-off area that turned a rear-wing failure into a race-deciding Safety Car at Stowe · Photo: Unsplash

The pit call that decided second: Russell stays out

Under the late Safety Car, Ferrari brought both Leclerc and Hamilton in to switch to the soft compound — a logical move with fresh rubber and nothing to lose on a shortened dash to the flag. Mercedes made the opposite call with Russell, keeping him out on track. With so few racing laps left and the pit-lane time loss still costing more than it could recover, staying out proved to be the correct read: Russell inherited and held second place, vaulting ahead of Hamilton, who rejoined third despite his fresher tyres.

No green flag: the FIA's software error and a finish nobody wanted

What followed produced the most contentious moment of the weekend. As lapped cars were waved through to unlap themselves ahead of a planned restart, a "Safety Car In This Lap" message appeared on Lap 51 — the standard signal that racing is about to resume. It never did. The race instead ran out under Safety Car conditions, denying the Silverstone crowd the green-flag dash to the line that the message had seemed to promise.

The FIA later confirmed the message had been a mistake. Under Article B5.13.5 of the Sporting Regulations, one full lap must be completed following the unlapping procedure before a restart can occur — a rule Race Control had, in fact, correctly applied throughout. The "Safety Car In This Lap" board, however, was displayed erroneously by a software error, creating the false impression among drivers, teams and the crowd that a final-lap shootout was imminent when procedurally it never could have been on that lap. Commentator and former F1 driver Martin Brundle was among those to publicly challenge the governing body over the finish.

"Displayed erroneously due to a software error."

— FIA statement, on the "Safety Car In This Lap" message shown on Lap 51

It's a strange echo of the sport's most infamous procedural controversy — Abu Dhabi 2021 — except in reverse. Where that finish stemmed from race control deviating from the written safety-car procedure to force a green-flag ending, Silverstone's confusion came from software incorrectly suggesting a restart was coming when the regulations never allowed for one on that lap. The FIA's automated unlapping system, built in the aftermath of exactly that 2021 controversy to remove human discretion from the process, produced its own new failure mode instead.

Championship arithmetic: Antonelli's lead cut to 25

Russell's second place — worth 18 points — combined with Antonelli's blank score to cut the championship gap from the 43 points Antonelli held after Saturday's sprint down to 25. Hamilton's third, worth 15 points even with the five-second penalty absorbed into his pit stop, moved him to within 32 points of the lead. It is comfortably the tightest the top of the standings has looked all season, and the first time since Antonelli's fifth win of the year at Monaco that Mercedes and Ferrari have had a genuine three-way title fight on paper rather than a single dominant storyline.

Antonelli's fastest lap of the race — set despite the wheel-shield failure and the eventual 16th-place finish — was the one small consolation from an afternoon that otherwise undid almost everything Saturday's sprint win had built. For Ferrari, the result matters far beyond one trophy: Leclerc's win, his first at Silverstone and his first anywhere since the United States Grand Prix in October 2024, is proof the pace the team found on Friday carries over full race distance, not just over a single qualifying lap or 17-lap sprint.
